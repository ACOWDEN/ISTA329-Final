Blockchains and Cryptocurrency

Refresher

By now, we've covered the main features of the systems that make our world go round today. We've highlighted that these systems are far from perfect, and that they come with important tradeoffs all of which are ultimately rooted in the need to trust other people. Simply put, we are always vulnerable to the bias, corruption, or neglect of whoever has control in the systems we use.

What is a Blockchain?

Try not to think too hard on the word “blockchain” itself, let's just talk the basics. Blockchains are a new way to build the trust-based systems in our lives, but trustlessly. If that seems a little confusing, let's use the Internet as an example. The Internet was a new a way to build the non-digital systems of our everyday lives, but digitally. Blockchains aren't trying to replace the Internet, but like the Internet, they are useful tools for radically improving the systems we use today with much better trade-offs.

The most basic (but not the only) function of a blockchain is to let us store and use digital money trustlessly. However unlike a bank or a digital payment provider, blockchains are not controlled by a single entity; they are decentralized. This design removes the single points of failure that we suffer from in the services we use today.

There is no system controller deciding who can and cannot use a blockchain, it's permissionless. Anyone can create a blockchain account, and no authority exists which can “ban” this account or control how it uses the blockchain. Users never have to worry about some system authority closing or freezing their account for any reason, and digital finance becomes vastly more accessible for anyone in the world with an Internet connection. Creating an account is functionally as simple as clicking a button, putting no personal information at risk since none is required.

With blockchain accounts and any assets stored within them, users get absolute ownership. Not only does no middleman exist with control over users' accounts, but users do not need a middleman to run the system they want to use. Instead, users can run the blockchain software directly on their own devices. This means with blockchains, we can make peer-to-peer (p2p) transactions with any other account 24/7, where there is no middleman in control of our account or the system, and therefore no middleman between us and the people we transact with.

Building Apps on a Blockchain

Now while it's great that anyone in the world can use a blockchain for storing and using digital money trustlessly, this is kind of boring. Sure, this covers simple financial services like banks and payment providers, but didn't we say that we can use blockchains to build any of the trust-based systems in our lives? Well, just like the Internet, we can build applications that use this core functionality of blockchains! By default, the blockchain doesn't offer some platform for trading stocks and commodities, taking out loans, or getting insurance, but these are apps that can be built on a blockchain. There is no built-in games or social media platforms, but these can be built on a blockchain too.
